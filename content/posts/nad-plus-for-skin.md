<p>Every function your skin performs requires energy. Repairing damage, producing collagen, fighting oxidative stress, maintaining barrier integrity, turning over cells, and healing after procedures all demand significant cellular fuel. When that fuel runs low, everything slows down. Recovery takes longer, aging accelerates, and skin loses its resilience and vitality.</p>

<p>This is where NAD+ enters the conversation. Nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide, known as NAD+, is not a trendy new discovery but a fundamental molecule that your cells have always depended on. Scientists have understood its importance in cellular biology for decades. What is new is the recognition that declining NAD+ levels contribute significantly to skin aging and impaired recovery, and that topical application can help address this decline.</p>

<p>Understanding NAD+ reveals why some skin simply functions better than others, why recovery slows as we age, and what we can do to support our skin at the most fundamental cellular level.</p>

<h2><strong>What Is NAD+?</strong></h2>

<p>NAD+ is a coenzyme present in every living cell of your body. It plays a central role in metabolism, converting the nutrients you consume into the cellular energy your cells need to function. Without adequate NAD+, your cells simply cannot perform their essential tasks efficiently.</p>

<p>At its core, NAD+ is a redox cofactor, meaning it participates in oxidation-reduction reactions that transfer electrons during metabolic processes. The practical implication is straightforward: NAD+ is essential for producing ATP, the energy currency your cells spend to do everything they do.</p>

<p>Beyond energy production, NAD+ serves as a critical substrate for several enzyme families that regulate cellular health. It is not simply fuel but also a signaling molecule that influences how your cells respond to stress, repair damage, and maintain their function over time.</p>

<p>The challenge is that NAD+ levels decline significantly with age. By middle age, your NAD+ levels may be half what they were in your youth. This decline correlates with many hallmarks of aging, including reduced cellular energy, impaired DNA repair, increased oxidative damage, and slower recovery from injury or stress.</p>

<h2><strong>How NAD+ Works in Your Skin</strong></h2>

<p>Understanding the specific mechanisms through which NAD+ benefits skin reveals why it is so valuable for both recovery and anti-aging applications.</p>

<h3><strong>Powering Cellular Energy Production</strong></h3>

<p>Your skin cells are constantly working. They produce proteins, maintain structures, fight pathogens, repair damage, and turn over regularly. All of this activity requires ATP, and NAD+ is essential for ATP production through cellular respiration.</p>

<p>When NAD+ levels are adequate, your skin cells have the energy they need to function optimally. When levels decline, cellular processes slow down. This is why aging skin often shows reduced collagen production, slower wound healing, and diminished barrier function. The cells are still capable of performing these functions, but they lack sufficient fuel.</p>

<h3><strong>Supporting DNA Repair Through PARPs</strong></h3>

<p>Your skin is constantly exposed to DNA-damaging stressors including UV radiation, pollution, and oxidative stress. Healthy skin relies on robust DNA repair mechanisms to fix this damage before it accumulates.</p>

<p>PARPs, or poly ADP-ribose polymerases, are enzymes critically involved in DNA repair. When DNA damage occurs, PARPs are activated to coordinate the repair response. The crucial point: PARPs require NAD+ as a substrate to function. Without adequate NAD+, PARP activity is limited, and DNA repair becomes less efficient.</p>

<p>This connection has significant implications for skin health. Accumulated DNA damage contributes to cellular dysfunction, premature aging, and increased risk of skin problems. Supporting NAD+ levels helps ensure your DNA repair machinery has the resources it needs.</p>

<h3><strong>Activating Sirtuins for Cellular Longevity</strong></h3>

<p>Sirtuins are a family of proteins often called longevity enzymes because of their role in regulating cellular health and lifespan. These enzymes influence numerous processes relevant to skin aging, including inflammation, oxidative stress response, and cellular senescence.</p>

<p>Like PARPs, sirtuins require NAD+ as a substrate. When NAD+ is abundant, sirtuin activity is supported. When NAD+ declines, sirtuin function diminishes, and many protective cellular processes become less effective.</p>

<p>Sirtuin activation has been linked to improved mitochondrial function, reduced inflammation, enhanced stress resistance, and delayed cellular senescence. By supporting NAD+ levels, you indirectly support this entire network of protective mechanisms.</p>

<h3><strong>Counteracting Cellular Senescence</strong></h3>

<p>Cellular senescence occurs when cells stop dividing and enter a dysfunctional state. Senescent cells accumulate with age and contribute to tissue dysfunction through the inflammatory signals they release. In skin, senescent cells contribute to thinning, reduced elasticity, impaired healing, and other signs of aging.</p>

<p>Research suggests that declining NAD+ levels contribute to the accumulation of senescent cells. By maintaining healthier NAD+ levels, you may help delay the onset of senescence and support more youthful cellular function over time.</p>
